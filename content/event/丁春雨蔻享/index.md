### 报告简介
Planetary near-surfaces form a critical interface linking morphology, internal structure, and environmental evolution. Leveraging planetary radars and multi-source observations from Chang’E-3/4/5/6, we present advances in radar processing, stratigraphic interpretation, and dielectric inversion—resolving 0–4 m regolith layering at Chang’E-3, quantifying heterogeneity, attenuation, and dielectric properties at Chang’E-4, and deriving in situ shallow profiles at the Chang’E-6 farside site to inform future base engineering and site selection. Extending this framework to Mars orbital datasets, we map near-surface structures and detect subsurface cavities, including a likely water-formed karst cave system in Hebrus Valles and candidate lava-tube skylights on the western flank of Elysium Mons, with quantified geometry, distribution, and thermal buffering. Together, these results demonstrate a closed-loop chain from radar observation and physical inversion to cavity detection and habitability assessment, providing quantitative support for Moon–Mars landing-site selection and subsurface space utilization.
